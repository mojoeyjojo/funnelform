# The Goal of This MVP

---

## The One-Sentence Goal

The goal of this MVP is to prove that a non-technical business owner will paste a URL, get an AI-generated quiz funnel they consider good enough to publish, publish it without any help from us, capture a real lead, and find that experience valuable enough to pay for.

Everything else is secondary. We are not trying to build a complete product. We are trying to validate a chain of assumptions, and each link in that chain is a thing that could be false.

---

## What This MVP Must Prove

This MVP is not a product launch. It is an experiment with a clear hypothesis, and the hypothesis breaks down into five specific claims that each have to be true for the business to work. The MVP exists to test all five as fast and as cheaply as possible.

**Claim one: the AI output is good enough to publish.** We have early evidence this is true from manual testing across real businesses, but we have not proven it inside a live product with real users who did not write the prompt. The MVP proves whether a stranger pastes their URL and looks at the result and thinks "yes, I'd put this on my site," versus "this isn't quite right." If most users need heavy rewriting, our entire value proposition shifts and we have a different, weaker product.

**Claim two: zero-touch onboarding actually works.** This is the riskiest assumption. The whole premise is that the AI removes the blank-page wall that causes most quiz-tool signups to give up before publishing. The MVP proves whether users actually reach the finish line on their own. The single metric that matters here is the percentage of signups who publish a quiz without us intervening. If that number is low, the product is not zero-touch in practice, no matter how good the generation is.

**Claim three: the lead capture and delivery loop closes.** A quiz that generates leads nobody receives is worthless. The MVP proves that a lead can flow from a quiz completion into the owner's hands, by email and by WhatsApp, reliably and without setup friction. This is where the WhatsApp wedge gets its first real test: do EU and LATAM users actually choose WhatsApp delivery, and does it remove the integration barrier as we predict?

**Claim four: people will pay.** Interest is not revenue. The MVP proves that users who reach the aha moment, a published quiz capturing real leads, convert into paying subscribers at a healthy rate when they hit the paywall. The reverse trial is the mechanism. The conversion rate is the verdict.

**Claim five: we can acquire users without paid spend.** The MVP proves that content and the free tool can bring the right people to the door. We are not testing whether we can buy growth. We are testing whether the organic, content-led motion produces qualified signups, because that is the only motion that scales for a solo founder on a tight budget.

---

## What This MVP Is Not Trying To Do

It is just as important to be clear about what we are deliberately not proving yet, because scope discipline is what makes the MVP fast.

We are not trying to serve every niche. Three niches, chosen for fit, is enough to test the model.

We are not trying to ship every integration. One native integration plus a universal fallback is enough to prove the delivery loop closes. The rest follow demand.

We are not trying to handle every edge case. A thin-website fallback and a single retry on bad AI output is enough robustness for a first cohort. Polish comes after validation.

We are not trying to build the full vision. No conversational funnels, no lead scoring, no multi-vertical platform. Those are real and they come later, but loading any of them into the MVP would slow us down and test nothing we do not already need answered first.

We are not optimizing conversion yet. We are establishing baselines. The founder is building this partly to master CRO, and you cannot optimize a funnel that does not exist. The MVP creates the funnel and the traffic. The optimization is the next chapter, not this one.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Success for this MVP is not a revenue number, although revenue is welcome. Success is learning, fast and cheaply, whether the five claims hold.

Concretely, within roughly ninety days of launch, success means: a clear majority of signups reach a published quiz on their own, real leads are flowing to real business owners through both email and WhatsApp, a healthy share of activated users convert to paid when they hit the paywall, organic content is producing a steady trickle of qualified signups, and a meaningful portion of EU and LATAM users are choosing WhatsApp delivery. If those things are happening, the model is validated and the job shifts from "does this work" to "how big can this get," which is where CRO, paid acquisition, and the broader vision take over.

Failure is also informative, and we want to know quickly. If users sign up but do not publish, onboarding is the problem. If they publish but do not convert, the value or the paywall is the problem. If they convert but we cannot acquire them, the channel is the problem. Each failure points at exactly one assumption to fix, which is the entire reason for building a tight MVP rather than a sprawling one.

---

## Why This Framing Matters

The trap with a product like this is building too much before learning anything. The category is full of feature-rich incumbents, and the instinct is to match them. That instinct is wrong for an MVP. Our advantage is not features, it is a sharp wedge: AI-first speed, a WhatsApp-and-localization play into an underserved market, and humane pricing. The MVP's only job is to prove that wedge is real with paying users.

So the MVP is deliberately narrow. One magic moment, made excellent. One activation path, made frictionless. One delivery loop that closes. One pricing model that converts. One acquisition channel that works without spend. Prove those, and we have earned the right to build everything else.

That is the goal: not to build the product, but to prove the product is worth building.
